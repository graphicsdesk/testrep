Last month, University President Lee Bollinger took the stage at the annual John Jay Awards Dinner to address students, alumni, and faculty of Columbia College.

He began his remarks by rattling off a few quick thank-yous, one of which went to outgoing dean of Columbia College, James Valentini.

Then something unexpected happened.

It was a moment that illustrated the unique position of the College dean in the Columbia ecosystem, as its most public-facing advocate. But the dean's is a job that may be changing—to the praise of some, and dismay of others—along with the curricular structure of the Faculty of Arts and Sciences as a whole.

It's the latest chapter in a saga that has been writing itself for decades, often in flurries of controversy several years apart.

TK: Restructuring something

By Zachary Schermele

April 18, 2022

Bollinger’s Task Force on the Relationship of the Arts and Sciences and the College, which was formed in September and has met virtually several times a month, has released a report recommending significant changes to the flow of administrative power.

ONE ILLO

Spectator has obtained eight documents, many of them confidential, dating back to October of last year, that were either sent to the task force or circulated within it.

On Oct. 27, leaders of the Columbia College Board of Visitors and Columbia College Alumni Association sent a letter to the task force urging them to continue to center the College dean in academic, student life, and alumni affairs.

A week later, Valentini sent his own letter to the task force appearing to challenge some of the narratives espoused by the groups seeking to change the power dynamics between Arts and Sciences and the College. One notable section illustrates the brute force of College alumni in the Columbia environment—and the dean's unique proximity to them.

In mid-January, a proposal was circulated among most of the members of what eventually became the de facto Faculty Subcommittee, which was formed rather organically after the president asked faculty to give a presentation to the rest of the task force toward the beginning of the process. The "new curriculum committee proposal" recommended the formation of a new mega-Committee on Instruction that would oversee large curricular decisions across Arts and Sciences. In this early version, the COI would not have been chaired by a faculty member, but rather would have been chaired jointly by the College dean and dean of the Graduate School of Arts and Sciences—a more similar construction to the current Executive Committee.

On Feb. 20, at the second to last meeting of the task force, an initial draft of recommendations that had been compiled by the Office of the President, with input from task force members and including the new COI proposal from the faculty, was debated.

 

One notable section of the draft allowed for the newly formed dean of Arts and Sciences to, along with the provost, be part of the president's determination of the salary of the College dean.

 

This initial draft was poorly received by a number of members, including Barnard President Sian Beilock; Thomas Cornacchia, a prominent financier and a member of the College BOV; and Brian Krisberg, a lawyer and former CCAA and CAA leader. At the meeting, Bollinger acknowledged the need for further meetings and another draft.

The final draft of the recommendations was distributed to task force members electronically on March 30, with a request to share feedback by April 4. The final report, which incorporated edits from several members, was sent to the entire task force on April 6, and made publicly available on April 8.

The final version pulls back from some of the changes proposed in the Feb. 20 draft by keeping the College dean at the center of admissions and financial aid discussions (although in the current construction of authority, the dean must already make enrollment decisions in conjunction with the executive vice president of Arts and Sciences, along with the provost and the executive vice president for finance).

Most notably, the final report punts on how to reconfigure the budgetary relationship between Arts and Sciences and the College, instead recommending the creation of a working group, which will center the new College dean and "examine budgetary challenges" over the next year.

 

The Feb. 20 document was much more radical—it even suggested a structure that would "centralize all development activities through Arts and Sciences and relieve the College of any responsibility to fundraise for its current-use budget."

One task force member took issue with the final recommendations. In a blistering six-page opinion, Brian Krisberg, CC ’81, Law ’84, dressed down the entire process, raising concerns about substance and process. He claimed the changes would "diminish authority significantly" of the College dean. Although his dissent was sent to the Policy and Planning Committee by the President's Office, the primary faculty governance body within Arts and Sciences, it was not distributed to the entire faculty along with the final report on April 11.
